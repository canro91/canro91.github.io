80% of the time, coding is a performance. The other 20% is doing real coding.

Performance means Scrum ceremonies, meetings, and JIRA:
* A meeting to present tickets for the next two weeks
* A meeting to answer questions from the previous meeting
* A meeting to watch someone enter a number in a text box. Read: poker planning and story points
* Meetings to watch someone move a ticket between JIRA lanes. Read: daily meetings
* A meeting about all the other meetings. Read: retrospective

And [when things go sideways]({% post_url 2022-12-17-LessonsOnAFailedProject %}), the performance intensifies with more frequent meetings. Sometimes a team member's only job is running ceremonies and writing reports.

That kills the fun of coding and can [make you hate it]({% post_url 2026-04-29-Coding %}).

This happens everywhere. Sylwia Laskowska asked in [a dev.to post](https://dev.to/sylwia-lask/is-software-development-just-a-side-quest-a-jira-story-4ng3) if that's something that only happens at her place. Nope! Even in the best families, as we say in our hometown.

Showing progress matters more than real work. Coding is often just a side quest. [A hard truth]({% post_url 2025-11-20-HardTruths %}) nobody tells us about.

To succeed as a coder, you need to master the ceremonies as much as the code. That's why [Street-Smart Coding](https://imcsarag.gumroad.com/l/streetsmartcoding/?utm_source=blog&utm_medium=post&utm_campaign=coding-living-is-performance-code) covers communication and collaboration. Because coding is more than typing symbols.

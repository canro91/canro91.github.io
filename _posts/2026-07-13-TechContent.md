Last week, I found this question on [a dev.to post](https://dev.to/dumebii/has-the-audience-for-technical-articles-dropped-5ceh):

> _"Is it just me, or has anyone noticed that articles on dev.to don't get as many reads/views as they used to before?"_

Yes! On dev.to, Medium, and on the Internet overall.

There's no point in writing how-to tutorials anymore, unless you're starting your coding or writing journey with [TIL posts]({% post_url 2025-04-10-TilPosts %}).

For step-by-step guidance on coding tasks, there's a magical text-area that seems to understands you and spits out answers fast.

Less people are landing on blogs and tutorials after Google pushed instant answers. For a couple of years, my series on [unit testing]({% post_url 2021-08-30-UnitTesting %}) and [LINQ]({% post_url 2021-01-18-LinqGuide %}) drove the most traffic. Even, [I went "viral"]({% post_url 2025-02-05-LessonsFromAViralPost %}) with a a post on [unit testing private methods]({% post_url 2024-11-30-TestingPrivateMethods %}). These days, they barely have any traffic.

And when was the last time you Google or open StackOverflow when stuck? Your behavior as reader tells a lot about what to do as a writer.

Is there still market for tech content? Yes.

But we have to give what AI can't. Thoughts, rants, stories...Authenticity. That's [how to stand out in a sea of AI slop]({% post_url 2025-02-15-FightingAIContent %}).

It's not a surprise that my most popular recent "technical" posts are [hard truths nobody told me about coding]({% post_url 2025-04-17-HarshTruths %}) and [the most painful lesson from my best job]({% post_url 2026-03-18-WorstJob %}).

If AI can do it in minutes, it isn't special.

_If you're starting out your coding journey, I still recommend you to write online. That's one of the lessons I cover in [Street-Smart Coding](https://imcsarag.gumroad.com/l/streetsmartcoding/?utm_source=blog&utm_medium=post&utm_campaign=re-audience-technical-articles-dropped)—The roadmap I wish I had when I started._
